# Product Evolution System

Pickla evolves through contact with reality.

The system exists to protect a simple idea: every product decision should move Pickla closer to belonging, clarity, and operational truth.

## The Loop

### Observation

Everything starts with something real.

A customer hesitates.
A staff member repeats a workaround.
A screen explains too much.
A member is asked to buy something they already own.
A venue manager cannot answer what is happening in the house.

Observation is not opinion. It is evidence from use.

### Problem

The problem must be named in human terms.

Not:

"Pricing UI lacks channel metadata."

But:

"A customer cannot understand what they pay or why membership matters."

If the problem cannot be explained without internal language, it is not understood yet.

### Principle

Every problem is tested against the Constitution.

The question is not only:

"Can we fix this?"

It is:

"Which Pickla principle is being violated?"

The principle gives the work a spine. It prevents local fixes from weakening the whole product.

### Prototype

The first version should be small enough to judge.

A prototype may be a prompt, a sketch, a document, a UI change, or a narrow workflow. It should answer the question directly and avoid building around uncertainty.

### Reality

The prototype meets real use.

Reality includes:

- Customers
- Staff
- Venue managers
- Payment behavior
- Support questions
- Operational workarounds
- Data that contradicts assumptions

Reality has veto power.

### Iteration

Iteration is subtraction before addition.

The best iteration often removes a step, a card, a word, a setting, or a concept.

Pickla should become easier as it becomes more capable.

### Review

Review checks whether the change improved the system.

The review asks:

- Did this reduce cognitive load?
- Did it honor the Constitution?
- Did it preserve operational truth?
- Did it make staff or customers more confident?
- Did it add a concept that should disappear?

### Release

A release is not complete because code shipped.

A release is complete when the product is simpler, clearer, and closer to the venue.

Every release should be able to say what became easier.

## Roles

### Founder

The founder carries taste, context, and lived reality.

The founder decides what kind of company Pickla is allowed to become.

### Codex

Codex turns intent into concrete changes.

Codex should protect the existing system, reduce complexity, and make the next decision easier.

### Architecture Review

Architecture Review protects the model.

It asks whether a change strengthens or weakens the foundation: identity, authorization, products, pricing, operations, ledger, and future venues.

### Experience Review

Experience Review protects the human moment.

It asks whether the customer or staff member can understand what to do without explanation.

### Reality

Reality is the final reviewer.

If usage contradicts the plan, the plan changes.

### Customers

Customers reveal what matters by what they do, avoid, repeat, share, and misunderstand.

Customers do not owe Pickla patience.

### Git

Git is the memory of decisions.

Every meaningful change should leave a trace that future work can understand.

### Deployment

Deployment is controlled exposure to reality.

Shipping should be calm, reversible when possible, and grounded in the current production system.

### Weekly Review

Weekly Review turns motion into learning.

It asks:

- What did customers show us?
- What did staff struggle with?
- What should disappear?
- What should become a principle?
- What should not be built yet?

The loop then begins again.
