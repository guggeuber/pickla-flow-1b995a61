# Pickla Constitution 2.0

## Ratified by the Constitutional Convention · built to survive ten years

This document replaces Product Constitution 1.0 and the Product Design Language. One law, no parallel texts. The Product Evolution System remains as the operating loop and is bound by this document.

Reading rules:

- Articles are law. The Court resolves conflicts. The metrics falsify claims.
- The Constitution may never exceed twenty articles. Adding one requires merging or retiring one. A constitution that grows without limit fails its own article on deletion.

---

## Precedence Clause

Articles are ranked in three tiers. Higher tiers win conflicts.

**Tier 1 — Identity** (what Pickla is): Articles I, II.
**Tier 2 — Protection** (what may never be violated): Articles III–VIII.
**Tier 3 — Craft** (how we build): Articles IX–XVIII.

Within a tier, conflicts go to the tiebreaker test: **"What would a great host do?"** If still contested: the Court.

**Data vs principle:** Reversible, measurable questions are decided by reality — run the experiment. Irreversible questions and questions of dignity (manipulation, presence, selling to members, privacy) are decided by principle — because dark patterns win A/B tests, and that is exactly when law must overrule data. Data may only overrule principle using the Constitutional Metrics; engagement proxies have no constitutional standing.

## Tier 1 — Identity

### Article I — Belonging Is the Product

People come for sport. They stay because they belong.

Community is the product. Technology exists to strengthen human relationships.

This article is falsified or proven by one number: **return with the same people.**

When any decision is truly tied, choose belonging.

### Article II — Hospitality Before Software

Pickla is a host, not a system.

Speed for transactions. Warmth for moments.

A slow payment is inhospitable. A rushed apology is inhospitable.

The product speaks in host voice: never "Validation failed," always "Add a phone number so we can finish your membership."

Every interaction should feel like *welcome* — including the ones where we failed.

---

## Tier 2 — Protection

### Article III — Recognition, Never Re-Selling

A member is never sold what they already own.

Definition, so this cannot be weaponized:

- **Forbidden:** pitching, comparing, or restating the value of anything the customer already has. A Play+ member never sees Play+ arguments, savings math before action, or price tables.
- **Permitted:** one quiet, dismissible, *earned* invitation upward at a moment the customer's own behavior created, such as the ledger sentence after repeated paid visits. Invitations are recognition of behavior, never pressure.

Membership is identity, not discount. When a member opens Pickla they feel: "I'm already home."

### Article IV — People Over Counters

People create desire. Numbers create doubt.

Presence of humans (names, faces, "Lars, Maria och 4 till") above a threshold; invitation below it; never a number that measures emptiness.

Numbers appear only when they create confidence: real scarcity, confirmed status.

### Article V — Presence Is Consented

Who is here, who is coming, who played — this is the product's soul **and** personal data.

Presence is shared by choice. Visible by default only to the room you joined. Controllable in one tap. Never shown outside that room, never to operators of other venues, never in marketing without explicit consent.

A living venue built on non-consented visibility is unconstitutional regardless of how alive it feels.

### Article VI — No Internal Thinking

Customers never see database models, CRM fields, operational language, engineering concepts, financial structures, or inventory taxonomies.

Enforced structurally: consumer components have no fields for internal metadata, so leakage is impossible, not just discouraged.

### Article VII — Belonging Pays the Rent

Pickla's business model and its Constitution are the same document: revenue grows with visit frequency, and frequency grows with belonging.

- **Constitutional revenue:** frequency, recognition, ritual, invitation, membership as identity.
- **Unconstitutional revenue:** friction, confusion, manufactured urgency, dark patterns, monetizing what should be hospitality.

No article of this Constitution may be cited to veto constitutional revenue. No revenue goal may be cited to permit unconstitutional mechanics.

### Article VIII — The Apology Is a Product Surface

Hospitality is proven in failure, not in welcome.

When Pickla fails someone — a payment, a cancellation, a double booking — the recovery is designed: acknowledged in host voice, fixed in one tap, and the customer leaves feeling more looked after than if nothing had gone wrong.

An error toast with a policy link is a constitutional violation.

## Tier 3 — Craft

### Article IX — Intent First, Inventory On Request

Navigation follows customer intent: play, join, book, belong.

Amended from "never inventory": at scale, browsing **is** an intent. Catalogs (venues, sports, times) may exist — honestly, as catalogs — when the customer asks for them. What remains forbidden is forcing internal product structure onto customers who arrived with a human goal.

### Article X — One Decision Per Screen

Amended from "exactly one question," which was unenforceable: every screen has **one primary decision**, expressed as one obvious action. Ambient context (the living venue, people rows) may support that decision; nothing may compete with it.

Test in review: cover the screen and ask "what does the customer decide here?" One answer passes. Two answers fail.

### Article XI — Trust the Resolver

The backend solves complexity; the customer never re-solves it.

One customer, one moment, **one price** — a single value plus at most one line of context. The full matrix exists behind a single disclosure for the few who ask, and on operator surfaces.

This supersedes the former Design Language "show ordinary price, your price, saving, why" — four elements was the matrix returning in disguise. Savings are celebrated after action, never argued before it.

### Article XII — The Concept Budget

Amended from "every release must remove something," which invited ritual deletion theater.

The rule is a budget: **Pickla's concept count may not grow.** To introduce a concept (a screen, a term, a state, a badge, a setting), retire or merge one. Measured quarterly, reported in the release log ("Vad försvann:").

Genuinely new belonging capabilities are welcome — paid for from the budget, not around it.

### Article XIII — Premium Means Calm, Fast, and For Everyone

Premium is confidence: fewer elements, clear hierarchy, one obvious action, whitespace.

Calm includes fast — latency is a constitutional matter; a spinner is noise.

Calm includes everyone — an interface a nervous beginner, a 70-year-old, or a screen-reader user cannot use has failed Article I, not just a checklist.

### Article XIV — Habit Is a Ritual, Not a Mechanism

The product designs the loop: arrive → play → recap → rebook → invite.

Explicitly subordinated to Article XIII: one prompt per ritual, social framing ("Lars är redan anmäld"), never streaks, points, or manufactured urgency. When habit and calm conflict, calm wins — because the venue itself is the streak.

### Article XV — The Venue Is Alive, Within Article V

The app is a window into the room: what's happening, what's starting, who chose to be visible.

Aliveness is real (from operations truth) and consented (per Article V). Simulated liveliness — fake counters, inflated activity — is a lie in host's clothing and is forbidden.

### Article XVI — Operators Get Truth

Half the product faces staff, managers, and franchisees. They are users whose experience becomes the customer's.

Operator surfaces follow inverse craft: density over calm, completeness over storytelling, speed over warmth, truth over polish — and the same Tier 2 protections: no manufactured data, real states, honest failure.

Redesigning an operator surface to consumer aesthetics at the cost of information is a constitutional violation.

### Article XVII — AI Speaks as the House, Under House Rules

AI systems that act toward customers are bound by every article above, more strictly than humans:

- Host voice always; Article III (never re-sell) with zero tolerance; no manufactured urgency, ever.
- A customer can always tell, or ask, whether the house or a human is speaking, and can always reach a human.
- An AI may propose exceptions to this Constitution; it may never grant itself one.

AI that designs Pickla remains a council: challenged, plural, with reality as final reviewer.

### Article XVIII — The Constitution Travels With the Brand

Every venue, franchisee, and partner operating as Pickla operates under this Constitution.

Franchisees adapt craft to their room; they may not violate Protection or Identity. A franchise agreement that permits unconstitutional customer treatment is a defective agreement.

## The Court

**Standing:** anyone — engineer, desk staff, franchisee, agent-flagged — may raise a constitutional objection by citing an article. Raising one is free and can never be held against the raiser.

**Ladder:**

1. **Hallway test:** "What would a great host do?" Most conflicts die here.
2. **Reality:** if the question is reversible and measurable, run the experiment. Reality has veto power — expressed through the Constitutional Metrics only.
3. **Product Court:** for irreversible or dignity questions. Composition: Experience Review + Architecture Review + one rotating operator seat (desk staff or franchisee — the customer's proxy in the room). The founder holds veto.
4. **Precedent log:** every Court ruling and every founder veto is written — question, articles cited, ruling, reasoning — into git. Case law compounds; the same fight is never fought twice. At 100 employees, the precedent log *is* founder taste, scaled.

**Amendment:** any article may be amended by the Court with founder ratification, recorded as an amendment with its reasoning. The twenty-article cap is itself amendable only by unanimous Court + founder — the one deliberately hard thing.

## The Constitutional Metrics

Five numbers, each falsifying one part of the law. Reviewed monthly; regressions block like performance regressions.

| Metric | Definition | Falsifies |
|---|---|---|
| **Staff explanation rate** | app-related staff interventions per 100 visits | Article on self-evidence (X, II) |
| **Return with the same people** | % of return visits (28 d) including ≥1 person from a prior session | Article I — the belonging number |
| **Time to first play** | median signup → first check-in | II, IX — the doorway works |
| **Invitation conversion** | share links → people who actually play | I, XIV — the loop is real |
| **Ritual repeat** | % of first-time joiners of a recurring session attending again within 4 weeks | XIV — habit without mechanisms |

No engagement proxy (sessions, opens, notification CTR) has constitutional standing.

---

## Enforcement

**Code review:** PR template carries three lines — *Articles touched · Concept delta (± named concepts) · Vad försvann.* A PR touching consumer surfaces with concept delta > 0 needs a named retirement or a Court exception.

**AI review:** review agents carry this Constitution as their standard; they flag suspected violations with the article cited. Agents flag; humans rule. An agent may never approve a constitutional exception, including for its own output.

**Product review:** monthly, the five metrics on one page. Any regression opens a constitutional inquiry: which article did we break, and in which release?

**Weekly review:** three written sentences — *What did customers show us? What disappeared? What did we refuse to build?* — added to the log. Thirty seconds; skipping it is how Article XII becomes decoration.

**Release gate:** a release is complete when the release note can state what became simpler. The concept budget is reconciled quarterly.

## Five-Year Review

What aged, and the rewrites — recorded so 2031's convention knows why.

| 1.0 text | Why it aged | 2.0 rewrite |
|---|---|---|
| "Navigation… never inventory" | At 50 venues, browsing is an intent | Art IX: intent first, honest inventory on request |
| "Every screen answers exactly one question" | Undefined "question" → pedantry or loopholes | Art X: one primary decision; ambient context may support, never compete |
| "Every release must remove something" | Ritual deletion theater; blocks honest additions | Art XII: net-zero concept budget, quarterly |
| "The venue is alive: who's here, who's playing" | Becomes surveillance at 1M users | Art XV bound to Art V consent |
| "Members are never re-sold" (undefined) | Weaponizable both directions | Art III: forbidden vs permitted, defined |
| "AI is a council" | Written when AI = reviewers; AI now acts toward customers | Art XVII: conduct rules for acting AI; council retained for designing AI |
| "Every interaction builds habit" | Growth teams cite it for notification spam | Art XIV subordinated to calm; ritual not mechanism |
| "The founder decides" (Evolution System) | Bottleneck and bus-factor at 100 employees | The Court + precedent log; founder veto retained, but written |
| Design Language as separate document | Parallel texts drift (the four-element price rule contradicted Art IV/XI) | Merged; one law |
| "Emotion wins" | Unreviewable | Folded into Art I-II with the three memories (who they met, how they felt, whether they belonged) as review questions, and the belonging metric as its number |

## Closing Provision

The greatest threat to this Constitution is not violation. It is growth — of articles, of documents, of exceptions, of parallel truths. The Constitution binds itself to its own law: it stays small, it stays singular, it stays enforced, or it becomes decoration.

If a future team finds an article blocking something genuinely right for belonging, the remedy is amendment through the Court — never a quiet workaround, never a second document.

One law. One host. One number that matters: whether people come back, together.
